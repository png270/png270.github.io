The Graden of Forking Paths Vs. An Onion

A tale about steganography + cryptography CTF challenge that I designed from scratch. Not that it tells you all about it, you just have to see it for yourself.

Published: June 08, 2026 • By: Siddhi

The garden of forking paths - This short story is my introduction to Borges and his art of metafiction and labyrinths. I stumbled across this short story while researching for ideas to connect the dots with my field of work.

I made a machine spit out some recommendations for me and this was one of them. It’s funny how we look up to machines even for ideas these days. “Ideas” which are inherently human.

One can argue this because what I did was basically get a machine to do my research for me while my idea solely belongs to me. However, this machine was instrumental in telling me the themes I should look out for, the dots I should ponder upon to form connections.

Yet the question remains – does this really make the idea human if I’m given the research I was supposed to do and the dots I was supposed to look for, on a silver platter? Mind you the platter is not silver but made of aluminum.

Few months ago, I used this aluminum platter to design a challenge that never made it out alive. It resembled Borges’ forking path in a form tangibly different such that it resembled an onion.

An onion is a weird pick. But hear me out. While it doesn’t fork into multiple possibilities, it resembles possibilities bundled into layers. You carefully peel apart one layer after another to reach the core of destiny, the final result. Forking paths or unraveling of layers, they both represent possibilities, one infinite, the latter finite.

The challenge embraces the art of steganography and then later delves into cryptography. A bulb of onion, carefully cut in half and placed on a strikingly white plate, is exposed with its core looking at you in disdain. Do we even know if this core gave life to an onion before it was an onion? This is what the picture asks of you, to find out if there’s something more.

So, you break it apart and embed an encrypted file in it. The file contains a secret recipe. This recipe leads you to your answer. But what if you can’t even open this file? Will the secret recipe, passed down from generations to next be lost forever?

You need to look carefully at each layer of onion. Maybe that is your key? The key to unlocking the recipe.

Wait, there’s more. It was just one layer.

Now you need to figure out correct portions of everything. But even when you figure it out, what does it produce? You’re one step away from your destiny, all you need to do is figure out this newspeak of a recipe. Once you do, you have your answers. Maybe not the answers you need in life.

Nonetheless, this is supposed to be about The Garden of Forking Paths by Borges. A short story that explores alternate realities. Borges describes a narrative centered around time, a word forbidden in the story written by our protagonist’s ancestor. It draws parallels from the way riddles are designed and concludes the metafictional concept of alternate universes. It draws your attention to the presence of forks in the road; the path you chose determines your outcome. While this discussion in the book plays out, I am directed to the opening of scene, Dr Yu Tsun’s internal monologue –

"I then reflected that everything that happens does so only to oneself and only now." — Jorge Luis Borges, The Garden of Forking Paths

This, in my opinion was what the story was about. While you may have multiple forks along the way, each one you chose will lead you to another divergence and each divergence will have you lead a journey that could be different than what you’re dealing with in the present. While you acknowledge this line of thinking, the only thing that matters to you is the present. I do not know how it relates to cybersecurity yet, but this is something very much relevant to the everyday life. What you do now is as irrevocable as what transpired. So, what’s the point in dwelling?
